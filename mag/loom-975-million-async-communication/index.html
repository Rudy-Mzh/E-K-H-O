Loom Was Acquired for $975 Million for a Video Messaging Tool. That Number Deserves Attention.

In 2023, Atlassian acquired Loom for $975 million. Behind that figure, a strong signal about the real cost of synchronous communication in organizations — and what companies are willing to pay to reduce it.

The problem Zoom didn't solve

The 2020 pandemic propelled Zoom to 300 million daily participants. It also revealed a problem that video conferencing amplifies rather than resolves: the tyranny of synchronicity. For a meeting to happen, 5 to 15 people must be available simultaneously, in the same timezone, with enough shared context for the conversation to be productive. This is not always possible, often suboptimal, and almost always expensive in aggregated time.

Loom asked a different question: how many of these meetings transmit information that could have been sent asynchronously, at any time, without imposing a time constraint on anyone?

The value of async video

A 3-minute Loom message can replace a 30-minute meeting. It can be viewed at any hour, in any timezone, at 1.5x speed. It can be replayed, paused, and shared with absent team members. It carries the tone of voice, facial expressions, and visual context that an email cannot convey. And unlike a meeting, it doesn't require the simultaneous availability of all participants.

One hour of meeting with 8 people at a $60 hourly cost costs $480. A 5-minute Loom message distributing the same information costs a few cents of storage and 5 minutes of recording. The economic equation is brutally clear.

Why Atlassian paid $975 million

Atlassian, publisher of Jira and Confluence, understood that asynchronous communication is the natural complement of its project management tools. Teams working in Jira need to communicate context without breaking their workflow to schedule a meeting. Loom integrates directly into this workflow: a developer records a 2-minute video explaining a bug, attaches it to the Jira ticket, and the product manager watches it the next morning at 8am before their first meeting. $975 million for a measurable reduction in communication friction in tech teams — one of the clearest acquisition calculations of the decade.
